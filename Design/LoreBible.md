# SECTION I: THE WORLD BEFORE THE EDICT

## The Age of Tusks (Pre-Edict Orc History)

### What the Orcs Were
Orcs were not a single nation. They were a continent-spanning collection of clan-states, each governed by a **Tuskthrone** — a chieftain who earned their seat by trial, not bloodline. Orc civilization was old. Older than the human kingdoms by centuries. They built in bone, stone, and living wood. Their cities were grown from forests shaped by shamanic blood-magic. Their written language was tattooed onto skin — history lived on the bodies of the people.

Orc magic — **inkcraft** — was biological. It derived from blood willingly given, mixed with mineral pigments from the deep earth, and etched into flesh. A tattooed orc wasn't decorated; they were *inscribed* — carrying spells, memories, and ancestral knowledge in their skin. A fully-inscribed elder was a walking library, a weapon, and a temple.

### The Clan Wars (The Reason the Needlewise Broke)
The five great clans were never at peace for long:

**The Hollow Hand** — Assassins, spies, diplomats. Believed that precision violence prevented mass violence. "One throat cut saves a thousand." They served whichever Tuskthrone hired them, maintaining a fragile balance of power. Smallest clan, most feared.

**The Irontusk** — Warriors, conquerors. Believed strength was the only honest currency. Expansionist. Responsible for three of the five Great Burnings (inter-clan genocides). They fought because fighting was how they proved they deserved to exist.

**The Ashroot** — Shamans, mystics, keepers of inkcraft. Neutral in theory, but they sold blood-ink to all sides, profiting from every war. They told themselves they were preserving knowledge. They were arms dealers.

**The Deepcradle** — Healers, caregivers, builders. The civilian infrastructure clan. They maintained the nurseries, the hospitals, the food stores. Every war devoured them first. They rebuilt every time. They were the strongest and most broken clan — endlessly repairing what the others destroyed.

**The Boneknit** — Crafters, miners, engineers. Built the great orc cities. Provided weapons to whoever paid. Pragmatic to a fault. They didn't care who won wars as long as contracts were honored.

### The Five Great Burnings
These were not skirmishes. These were extinction-level inter-clan wars.

1. **The First Burning** (approx. 400 years pre-Edict) — Irontusk vs. Hollow Hand. The assassins killed three Irontusk Tuskthrones in a single night. Irontusk responded by burning every Hollow Hand settlement they could find. Estimated dead: 40,000 orcs.

2. **The Second Burning** (approx. 300 years pre-Edict) — Irontusk vs. Ashroot. A dispute over inkcraft monopoly. Ashroot refused to tattoo Irontusk warriors. Irontusk sacked the Ashroot's sacred groves and forcibly extracted blood-ink from captured shamans. The survivors developed the first "curse-inks" — tattoos designed to kill the bearer. Estimated dead: 70,000 orcs.

3. **The Third Burning** (approx. 200 years pre-Edict) — All clans. A succession crisis where three Tuskthrones claimed the same territory. Lasted 40 years. The Deepcradle was nearly wiped out. They stopped rebuilding cities after this one. What was the point?

4. **The Fourth Burning** (approx. 120 years pre-Edict) — Irontusk vs. everyone. The most aggressive Tuskthrone in orc history, **Gorran the Uncarved** (who refused tattoos, believing ink was weakness), attempted to unite all clans by force. He nearly succeeded. His atrocities are what convinced Mother Ashgrove that the cycle would never end.

5. **The Fifth Burning** (approx. 80 years pre-Edict) — The last inter-clan war. A brief, vicious conflict interrupted by the first human incursions into orc territory. The clans united temporarily against the external threat — but Ashgrove had already made her decision.

### Mother Ashgrove's Calculus
She was High Shaman of the Ashroot during the Fourth and Fifth Burnings. She personally buried children from every clan. She watched Gorran the Uncarved stack orc skulls into monuments. She saw the Deepcradle healers stop crying — not because they'd healed, but because they'd run out of tears.

When the human kingdoms offered an alliance against "the common orc threat" (really a land grab), she saw an opportunity. Not a betrayal — a *mercy*. She designed the Edict using Ashroot's deepest inkcraft knowledge, wove it into a curse that would be sustained by human blood, and gave it to the six kingdom founders willingly.

Her reasoning: *If orcs are too weak to fight, they cannot fight each other. If they fade slowly, they fade without the screaming.*

She was wrong. The humans turned weakness into slavery. Slow decline became active cruelty. But by then the Edict was locked, and she couldn't undo it alone.

# SECTION II: EXPANDED CHARACTER PROFILES

## Vetch — Full Background

### Early Life
Born in a temporary camp in the Ashwild volcanic wastes. Mother: **Serrit**, a Deepcradle healer. Father: **Dorn**, a Hollow Hand operative who died on a reconnaissance mission when Vetch was six — killed by Greenhold border guards. Vetch was told their father "went to scout and didn't come back." The full truth (that Dorn was captured, tortured for information, and displayed on a scarecrow frame in a wheat field) is something Vetch discovers during Kingdom 1.

### The Personal Stake — Lorne
**Lorne** is Vetch's younger sister. She's 22 — young for an orc, but the Edict has aged her body to the equivalent of 50. Her bones are brittle. She coughs blood most mornings. She was a promising Boneknit apprentice — a crafter of delicate bone instruments — but her hands shake too badly now. She still tries.

Lorne is not a fighter. She's sharp-tongued, funny in a bleak way, and she makes Vetch laugh — the only person who can. She stays at camp. She's the reason Vetch comes back.

**Narrative function:** Lorne is the ticking clock. If the Edict isn't broken, she dies. Not in the abstract. *Her.* She gets worse between each kingdom. By Act 4, she can't walk. By Act 5, she's bedridden. In the final choice, she's in the room.

### Training
Recruited into the Hollow Hand at age 12 by **Old Thresh**, the last Hollow Hand master. Trained in silence-movement, blade work, poison craft, and observation. The Hollow Hand were never many — at their height, perhaps 200 operatives. Now there are two: Vetch and Old Thresh. Thresh is 58, half-blind, and his hands tremble. He won't survive another winter. He gave Vetch the final mission briefing and said: *"We were always the scalpel. Everyone else was the hammer. Prove we were right."*

### Personality — Deeper
Vetch's quiet isn't stoicism — it's *listening*. They were trained to hear the space between words. In dialogue, this manifests as Vetch noticing things other characters miss: a lie in someone's tone, a guard who favors one leg, a door that's been opened recently.

They have a dry, cutting humor that surfaces unexpectedly. Stress makes them funnier. This is a defense mechanism Lorne taught them.

They struggle with the tattoos. Each one is a violation of Hollow Hand philosophy — the Hand believed in leaving *no mark*. No trace. No evidence. Now Vetch's body is a canvas of evidence. The irony isn't lost on them.

## The Needlewise (Mother Ashgrove) — Full Background

### Before the Edict
Born **Ashgrove** (single name, as is Ashroot tradition) approximately 180 years ago. Orc lifespan was naturally 150-200 years before the Edict; she's old even by pre-Edict standards, sustained by deep inkcraft that slows her aging.

She was brilliant. The most gifted shaman the Ashroot produced in generations. She could read ink — touch a tattooed orc and know their entire history. She could write ink that carried emotions, that healed wounds, that made crops grow.

She fell in love once. A Deepcradle healer named **Moth**, who was killed during the Fourth Burning when Gorran's army overran a field hospital. Moth was unarmed, treating wounded from both sides. Gorran's soldiers didn't care. Ashgrove found the body three days later, still warm — the healing inks on Moth's skin were trying to keep the flesh alive.

That was the day she started designing the Edict.

### The Deal
She met the six human kingdom founders in secret. They were minor lords at the time — hungry, ambitious, eager for orc land. She offered them a weapon. In exchange, she asked for one thing: that orcs never be given weapons again. She naively believed the humans would simply *leave the orcs alone* once they were no longer a threat.

The humans agreed. Then they enslaved the weakened orcs. Ashgrove went into hiding. She told herself that slavery was still better than the Burnings. She told herself that for a hundred years.

### As the Needlewise
She re-emerged thirty years ago, when a young orc named Rukh found her in a mountain cave, half-dead from self-inflicted ink experiments. She told him she was "a Needlewise — a keeper of the old ways." She never gave her real name.

She offered to help the resistance. She understood the Edict because she *made* it — she could design counter-inks that weaponized its power. She couldn't undo it alone (it requires killing all Edictbearers), but she could arm the one who would.

She chose Vetch because of the Hollow Hand philosophy: precision over brutality. If any orc would carry the Edict's power without becoming Gorran, it would be a Hand.

### The Contingency
What she hasn't told anyone: the tattoos she's designing carry a hidden layer. If the final Edictbearer dies and all ink is absorbed, Ashgrove can activate a command-sequence in the tattoos that makes the Edict *permanent but painless* — a slow, comfortable extinction rather than the current agony. This is Ending B. She sees it as the most loving option. "Let them go gently."

But she's not certain anymore. That's the truth. She started this plan sure she was right. Now, watching Vetch, watching Lorne, watching the camp's children play — she doesn't know.

### Tells & Foreshadowing (In-Game)
- She knows the botanical names of plants from Greenhold, the Drench, and the Verdancy — places she shouldn't have visited.
- She sometimes slips into archaic Ashroot grammar that predates the current generation.
- When Vetch describes Edictbearer behavior, she nods like she's *confirming*, not learning.
- She is physically uncomfortable around Irontusk orcs. Old trauma.
- Her tattoos (which she claims are "practice marks") are in a script no living orc can read. Because it's the original Edict-script.

## Rukh — Full Background

### Origin
Born into the Irontusk clan 42 years ago. Built like a siege engine — broad, heavy, with hands like stone mallets. His tusks were the last generation to grow full-length, and he's fiercely proud of them.

He was a raider. Not apologetic about it. The Irontusk believed in taking what you needed, and Rukh was good at it. He hit supply convoys between human kingdoms, stealing food and medicine for the camps. He was fast, brutal, and effective.

### The Crippling
At age 30, the Edict accelerated in his legs. Over six months, his bones thinned, his knees swelled, and his legs simply stopped bearing his weight. He went from the fastest raider in the Ashwild to a man who can't stand without crutches.

The rage almost killed him. He drank cave-water spirits until his liver nearly failed. Senna saved his life. He hates that he needed saving.

### Handler
Rukh rebuilt himself as an intelligence operative. He can't move, so he makes the world move around him. His spy network — a handful of orcs disguised as human servants, laborers, and beggars embedded in each kingdom — is the resistance's only advantage.

He's brilliant tactically. He reads people the way Vetch reads rooms. He identified the Edictbearers, mapped their habits, and built the target profiles Vetch uses.

### Relationship with Vetch
He respects Vetch but doesn't fully understand the Hollow Hand way. To Rukh, killing should be direct — a blade in the chest, eyes open, both parties knowing what's happening. The Hollow Hand's precision feels like cowardice to his Irontusk upbringing, even though his rational mind knows it's effective.

He and Vetch have a ritual: after each mission, Vetch sits with Rukh at the map table and recounts every kill in clinical detail. Rukh listens without judgment. Then he pours two cups of cave-water spirits — one for Vetch, one for himself — and they drink without speaking. It's the closest either of them gets to processing what's happening.

### What Breaks Him
The revelation about the Needlewise. Rukh *found* her. He brought her to camp. He vouched for her. Learning that the woman he trusted — the woman whose ink is *inside* Vetch — engineered the orc decline is a betrayal he processes as personal failure. He chose wrong. His judgment failed. For a man who rebuilt his identity on his mind after his body broke, this is annihilation.

## Deepcradle Senna — Full Background

### Origin
Born Deepcradle, 35 years old. One of the last orcs trained in traditional Deepcradle medicine — a system of herbal remedies, bone-setting, and minor blood-magic that kept orcs alive for millennia.

She is small for an orc. The Edict hit Deepcradle hardest because their bloodline was already refined for sensitivity — healers needed to feel their patients. Now that sensitivity means she feels the Edict in her own bones constantly. A low hum of wrongness. She's adapted to functioning through chronic pain so completely that she doesn't mention it. Others sometimes notice she grips tables when she thinks no one's looking.

### Personal History
She had a daughter. **Pip.** Born three years ago with bones so thin they fractured during delivery. Pip lived for eleven days. Every one of those days, Senna held her without putting her down, because putting her down might break something else.

Senna doesn't talk about Pip. There's a small bone rattle in her shelter — Boneknit crafted, meant for a baby's hand — that she hasn't moved. Vetch notices it but doesn't ask. (If the player examines it during a camp scene, Senna says only: "She would have been a good crafter. She had clever hands.")

### Role in Camp
Senna is the only one who can assess the tattoo damage. Each time Vetch returns from a kingdom, she catalogs the changes: accelerated heart rate, shifting bone density, skin that's slightly wrong in color or texture, the whispers.

She's clinically honest with Vetch. "Your blood pressure is higher than any orc I've measured. Your skin has micro-fractures along the tattoo lines where the ink is restructuring the dermis. You are becoming *something*, and I don't know what."

She's terrified of the tattoos. They represent the same inkcraft that (she believes) was lost a century ago. The fact that they work — that they carry power — means the old knowledge is real. And the old knowledge includes weapons. Senna is a healer. The idea that healing and killing come from the same ink is something she cannot reconcile.

### Relationship with Lorne
Senna is Lorne's primary caregiver. They've developed a quiet friendship. Lorne makes Senna laugh — which almost nobody can do. Senna braids Lorne's hair when Lorne's hands are too shaky to do it herself. It's the closest thing to tenderness in the camp.

## Elder Grael — Full Background

### Origin
Irontusk. Age 51 — ancient for a post-Edict orc. He should be dead. The fact that he isn't is a mystery even to Senna; she suspects his body is so saturated with old battle-scars that the Edict can't find clean tissue to degrade. He's more scar than orc.

He fought in the last orc resistance — a doomed uprising 30 years ago where 200 orcs attacked a Greenhold border fort. Fourteen survived. Grael killed eleven humans with a stone axe before a crossbow bolt took his left eye.

### Philosophy
Grael doesn't believe in stealth. He thinks the Hollow Hand approach is a sickness — a willingness to let others die slowly while you wait for the "perfect moment." He wants open war. Not because he thinks they'd win — he knows the numbers — but because he believes orcs should die fighting, not fading.

"The Edict takes our children in their sleep. I say we take their children while they're awake. At least then someone *remembers* us."

He's wrong, and he knows it. But he's dying, and he wants to die correctly.

### Relationship with the Camp
He represents about 40% of the camp's population — the Irontusk and Boneknit orcs who want action, not patience. He doesn't undermine Vetch's missions, but he makes it clear that if the assassination approach doesn't produce results, he'll rally his people and attack the nearest kingdom. It will be a massacre. It will be on Vetch's head.

### Redemption Possibility
If Vetch completes Kingdoms 1-3 successfully, Grael's attitude shifts. He doesn't become gentle — that's not in him — but he begins to see the wisdom. "You're not a coward, Hand. You're just quiet about your killing. I can respect quiet killing, as long as the killing happens."

He offers Vetch something in Act 4: his blessing. In Irontusk tradition, an elder's blessing is a physical act — Grael presses his forehead against Vetch's and growls a single word in old Irontusk: "**Krath**" (rough translation: "the one who earns their scars"). It's the closest thing to a father's approval Vetch has received since Dorn died.

## Lorne — Full Background

### Who She Is
Vetch's younger sister. 22 years old. Boneknit apprentice — she chose Boneknit over Hollow Hand because she wanted to *make* things, not unmake them. She carves delicate instruments from bone: flutes, needles, measuring tools, lock mechanisms. Her work is precise and beautiful.

The Edict is killing her faster than most because she inherited their mother Serrit's Deepcradle bloodline sensitivity. Her bones are thinning. Her lungs fill with fluid some mornings. Her hands — her beautiful, clever hands — shake.

### Personality
Lorne is the funniest person in the camp, and she weaponizes it. She makes jokes about dying because if she doesn't, she'll scream. Her humor is dark, fast, and aimed at everyone equally, including herself.

"How's the mission going, Vetch? Kill anyone important? No? Just another Tuesday of stabbing minor bureaucrats? Very heroic."

She's also the most emotionally honest character. She says what everyone is thinking. When the Needlewise gives a cryptic answer, Lorne says: "That's not an answer, grandmother. Try again with words that mean things."

### Relationship with Vetch
She is the only person who calls Vetch by a childhood nickname: **"Vet."** Nobody else is allowed. Vetch once broke a man's finger for trying.

Their dynamic: Lorne teases, Vetch absorbs. Lorne pushes, Vetch steadies. She is angry about dying, and she takes it out on Vetch because Vetch is the only person strong enough to take it without breaking.

She's also proud of Vetch in a way she can't articulate. She carves a small bone token for each kingdom Vetch completes — an animal from the region's fauna, detailed and tiny. She leaves them on Vetch's bedroll without comment.

### Narrative Function
Lorne is the cost made visible. Every time Vetch returns to camp, Lorne is slightly worse. The player can't ignore this. The Edict isn't an abstract problem — it's Lorne's hands shaking, Lorne's laugh turning into a cough, Lorne's bone carvings getting less precise as her fingers fail.

In the final choice, Lorne is present. She doesn't tell Vetch what to do. She says: "Whatever you pick, Vet. I trust you. I just—" and then she coughs, and doesn't finish.

## Old Thresh — Vetch's Master

### Background
The last Hollow Hand master. Age 58. Taught Vetch everything. His body is failing — the Edict has attacked his eyes (nearly blind), his joints (can barely hold a blade), and his throat (speaks in a rasp).

He gave Vetch the mission and stayed behind. He mans the camp's perimeter using hearing alone — he can't see intruders, but he can hear a heartbeat in a still room. The few times intruders have found the camp, Thresh killed them by sound.

He doesn't appear often, but when he does, it matters. His approval is the rarest currency in the game. A nod from Thresh means more to Vetch than any tattoo.

### Key Dialogue
After the Needlewise reveal, Thresh is the only one who isn't surprised. Vetch asks why. Thresh says: "I'm Hollow Hand, boy. I knew she was lying from the first day. Her breathing changed every time someone mentioned the Edict."

"Why didn't you say anything?"

"Because her ink works. And I needed you alive more than I needed the truth."

# SECTION III: EXPANDED KINGDOM LORE

## Kingdom 1: The Greenhold

### History
Founded 90 years ago on conquered orc grasslands — specifically, the ancestral grazing grounds of the Irontusk clan. The soil is rich because generations of orc boneknit-crafters composted it with mineral amendments. The humans don't know this. They believe the land is "blessed."

The first Edictbearer, **Aldric Cowl**, was a cattle rancher who saw the weakening orcs as a labor opportunity. He penned them like livestock. His descendants have refined the system into something that looks almost civilized — the orcs "work for room and board." The rooms are pens. The board is gruel.

### Culture & Daily Life
The Greenhold is aggressively idyllic. Flower boxes on every window. Children playing in cobblestone streets. Harvest festivals with music and dancing. The cruelty is underground — literally. The orc labor camps are beneath the wheat fields, connected by tunnels. Aboveground, no orc is visible. The humans have made suffering invisible.

**Religion:** They worship the god of plenty, **Auros**, depicted as a golden bull. Harvest prayers include thanks "for the strong backs that till" — an oblique reference to orc labor that most citizens don't consciously connect to real beings.

**Food:** The Greenhold exports grain and cattle across all six kingdoms. Their economic power is foundational — everyone eats Greenhold bread. This means everyone is complicit.

**Architecture:** Whitewashed stone, thatched roofs, golden weathervanes. Pastoral perfection. The contrast with the underground camps is the point.

### The Scarecrows
The most disturbing detail: the "scarecrows" in the wheat fields are orc skeletons mounted on wooden frames. The practice started as intimidation — warning other orcs to stay away. Over generations, it became tradition. Farmers maintain their family's scarecrow skeletons with pride. Children name them. There's an annual "Scarecrow Festival" where the best-maintained skeleton wins a prize.

Nobody in the Greenhold sees this as horrifying. That's what makes it horrifying.

### Key Locations — Expanded
- **Goldmanor:** Lord Cowl's estate. Three stories, 40 rooms, servant quarters (human), private chapel, underground passage to the labor camps. Cowl hosts weekly dinners for the local gentry. These are potential infiltration opportunities — disguise as a servant, poison in the wine cellar, ambush during the after-dinner hunt.
- **The Underfields:** Tunnel network connecting eight orc labor camps. Approximately 200 orcs are held here. Conditions are survivable but degrading — no sunlight, minimal medical care, forced breeding quotas (the Greenhold is trying to maintain its labor supply despite the Edict's fertility effects). Liberating the Underfields is an optional objective that dramatically increases Kingdom 1's alert level but provides Irontusk recruits for the camp.
- **The Mill Quarter:** Where grain is processed. Heavily guarded because it's the economic heart. Contains the kingdom's treasury and communications hub.
- **Cowl's Chapel:** A private shrine where Harlan Cowl prays every dawn. He's alone for exactly twelve minutes. This is the assassination window.

### Target Expansions

**Lord Harlan Cowl (Edictbearer)** — More detail:
Harlan is 54, heavyset, with a booming laugh and a genuine love of farming. He wakes at dawn, prays, inspects his fields personally, and eats supper with his family every night. He's a *good father*, a *generous neighbor*, and a man who has never once visited the Underfields because "the overseers handle that sort of thing."

He's not a monster. He's a man who inherited a system and never questioned it. That's worse.

His blood carries the strongest single Edict-anchor (as first-family Edictbearer). Killing him weakens the curse by roughly 25%.

**Dame Cowl (optional target)** — More detail:
**Elara Cowl**, née Ashford (yes — she's from the Verdancy's Ashford hunting family). She married Harlan for political alliance. She's sharper than her husband. She runs the estate's spy network, tracking resistance movements.

*But:* She shelters three orc children in a hidden room in the manor's east wing. Orphans from the labor camps. She teaches them to read. She doesn't know why she does this — it started with one child she couldn't watch die, and now there are three, and she's terrified of being discovered.

Killing her collapses Harlan's intelligence network (making him easier to reach) but kills the only human actively saving orc children. The three children are relocated to the labor camps if she dies. If the player discovers the hidden room first...choices.

## Kingdom 2: The Drench

### History
Originally a fishing village. Became wealthy after discovering deep-sea pearl beds 70 years ago. The pearls are unique — they contain trace amounts of a mineral that interacts with the Edict, acting as curse-amplifiers when worn by Edictbearers. Admiral Keelan's pearl pendant doesn't just focus his Edict-anchor; it *broadcasts* it, extending his influence across coastal waters.

The first orc divers were volunteers — refugees desperate for any paying work. When the Edict weakened them enough that they started drowning, the Drench simply switched to conscription.

### Culture & Daily Life
The Drench is beautiful in a decaying way. The city is built on stilts and pontoons over a lagoon. Everything is damp. Wood rots, metal rusts, fabric molds. There's a saying: "Nothing lasts in the Drench, so enjoy it while it floats."

**Caste system:** Society is divided into Drylanders (the wealthy, who live on the upper platforms), Stilt-Folk (workers on the mid-levels), and Wetters (laborers on the lowest platforms, constantly flooded). Orcs are below Wetters — they're "Undertow."

**Currency:** Pearls serve as both currency and status symbols. The size, color, and luster of your pearl necklace communicates your rank. The irony — that this wealth is built on orc drownings — is the kind of thing that everyone knows and nobody says.

**Rain:** It rains constantly. Not a drizzle — sheets of water. This affects gameplay: rain masks sound (good for stealth) but makes surfaces slippery (bad for climbing). It also means everyone wears heavy rain cloaks, which are excellent as disguises.

### The Diving Pens
Orc slaves are kept in underwater cages anchored to the seafloor. They're hauled up for dives, pushed down with weighted belts, and expected to harvest pearls until their lungs give out. Average survival: seven dives. The Edict has thinned their lung tissue so badly that water pressure alone can kill them.

Surgeon Hask's "modifications" — gills cut into necks, lungs punctured and cauterized to equalize pressure — extend survival to maybe twenty dives. The modified orcs are in constant agony. Hask considers this a medical triumph.

### Target Expansions

**Admiral Voss Keelan (Edictbearer):**
Age 47. Lean, precise, cold. A naval strategist who fought actual pirates before turning to the more profitable business of pearl extraction. He views everything — people, ships, weather — as logistics.

He doesn't hate orcs. He doesn't feel anything about orcs. They're a line item. "Cost per dive, yield per diver, replacement rate." When an advisor once suggested improving conditions to extend diver lifespan, Keelan ran the numbers and concluded that "replacement is cheaper than maintenance."

His pearl pendant is the key to his Edict-anchor. Destroying or taking the pearl weakens him significantly before the kill.

He lives on his flagship, *The Undrowned* — a massive war-galley anchored in the central lagoon. He rarely comes ashore. Getting to him requires either infiltrating the ship or luring him onto land.

**Wrack (the orc turncoat):**
Born **Wreth**. Years ago, she was a diver who survived longer than anyone expected. When the guards noticed, they offered her a deal: manage the other divers, and you never dive again. She agreed.

She's been managing orc slaves for four years. She's efficient at it. She doesn't beat them — she doesn't need to. She simply assigns dive schedules, delivers equipment, and reports problems. The humans consider her a model employee.

She hates herself so comprehensively that she's stopped feeling it. She sleeps well. She eats well. She watches orcs drown and notes the time in a logbook.

The player choice with Wrack: Kill her (she's complicit), spare her (she's a victim), or recruit her (she has intimate knowledge of the Drench's infrastructure). If recruited, she becomes an asset in the camp but a deeply uncomfortable presence — other orcs despise her, and she accepts their contempt as earned.

## Kingdom 3: The Fane of Flensing

### History
Founded as a monastery 120 years ago by **Scholar-Lord Myre I**, a human alchemist who became obsessed with the Edict's biological mechanism. He realized that studying the curse's effects on orc tissue could yield medical breakthroughs — and he was right. The Fane's research has produced treatments for human bone disease, blood disorders, and aging. Every kingdom's hospitals use Fane-derived medicine.

The cost has been approximately 8,000 orc lives over 120 years. The Fane keeps meticulous records. Every specimen is numbered.

### Culture & Daily Life
The Fane is a research institution that became a city. Everything is organized, labeled, and climate-controlled. The architecture is white stone and glass — designed to maximize light in the laboratories. It's built into a glacier, so the temperature is perpetually cold. The residents wear wool and fur-lined robes.

**Hierarchy:** Scholar-ranks, from Apprentice to Provost. Advancement is by published research. The most prestigious publications involve orc tissue studies. Young scholars compete for access to the specimen labs.

**Ethics:** The Fane has an ethics board. It meets quarterly. Its proceedings are confidential. It has never blocked a research proposal.

**The Lens Network:** Every corridor has crystal-lens cameras — alchemically powered surveillance devices. Lensmaster Obel monitors them from a central observation room. This makes the Fane the hardest kingdom to stealth through. The lenses must be disabled, avoided, or fed false images.

### Target Expansions

**Apprentice Davan:**
Age 24. Assigned to Dr. Carrow's vivisection lab six months ago. She entered the Fane believing in medicine, in helping people. She published a minor paper on herbal anesthetics that caught Carrow's eye.

Her first day in the specimen lab, she watched Carrow open a living orc's chest cavity to observe Edict-degradation on the lungs in real time. The orc was conscious. Davan vomited. Carrow said, "You'll adapt."

Davan hasn't adapted. She's documenting everything Carrow does — not to report it (to whom?), but because she needs to believe that someone, someday, will read her notes and *understand what happened here*.

If Vetch contacts Davan (possible through stealth and dialogue), she can become an informant: unlocking security routes, disabling lenses, and providing the archive's access codes. She asks for one thing in return: "Get me out. Not eventually. *Now.*"

If Vetch kills Davan instead (she is technically complicit — she hasn't stopped the vivisections), her journal can still be recovered. It contains the same intel, but no ongoing support.

## Kingdom 4: The Verdancy

### History
The oldest of the six kingdoms. The Ashford family has held these forests for 200 years — they were the first humans to encounter orcs, and they've been hunting them the longest. Originally, orc hunting was territorial defense. Then it became sport. Then it became culture. Then it became religion.

The Verdancy provides timber, leather, and "game management services" (orc hunts) to the other kingdoms. Wealthy humans from all six kingdoms travel here for guided hunts. It's a tourism industry built on murder.

### Culture & Daily Life
Forest people. They live in treehouses, lodges, and hunting camps. Everything is wood, leather, and antler. They're self-sufficient — they grow little, hunt everything. Their tracking skills are extraordinary.

**The Hunt Code:** A formal set of rules governing orc hunts. It has weight limits (no killing orc children under a certain size), seasonal restrictions (no hunting during orc breeding season — not out of mercy but to maintain the "stock"), and trophy guidelines (which bones can be taken, how to mount them). The fact that a *code of ethics* exists for hunting sentient beings is the Verdancy's particular brand of evil.

**Dogs:** The Verdancy breeds **Gnaw-hounds** — large, intelligent tracking dogs bred specifically to smell orc blood. They're the primary stealth-counter in this kingdom. Killing dogs raises alarm. Avoiding dogs requires scent-masking (ash, river-mud, certain herbs) that is kingdom-specific gameplay.

### Target Expansions

**Huntmaster General Brielle Ashford (Edictbearer):**
Age 50. Athletic, scarred, patient. She's tracked and killed more orcs than any living human — her personal count is 47. She keeps a tally notched into her bow.

She doesn't consider herself cruel. She considers herself a *conservationist*. In her worldview, orcs are a declining species that needs "management." The hunts prevent overpopulation in the preserves (where overpopulation is impossible because the Edict suppresses breeding). She's built an elaborate self-justification, and she believes it completely.

She is the only Edictbearer who will *hunt Vetch back*. Once she becomes aware of an assassin in her kingdom, she doesn't retreat to a safe room — she grabs her bow and enters the forest. She's the final boss of Kingdom 4, and the fight is a cat-and-mouse through dense woodland where she's at home and Vetch is the trespasser.

She respects skill. If Vetch demonstrates exceptional stealth (completing Kingdom 4 with low detection), her dying words change. High detection: "Clumsy beast." Low detection: "I never heard you coming. *Good.*"

**Lady Ashford the Younger (Brielle's daughter):**
**Mira Ashford**, age 21. She hunts because her mother expects it. She's good at it — she's an Ashford — but she throws up after every kill. She hides this.

She's an optional target. Killing her is mechanically beneficial (weakens Brielle's intelligence network) but morally complicated — she's the closest thing to a redeemable person in the Verdancy. If spared, she appears in the Accord Spire in Kingdom 6, having fled her mother's legacy. She can provide information or serve as a narrative foil.

## Kingdom 5: The Crucible of the Named

### History
The youngest kingdom, founded 70 years ago by a charismatic preacher named **Father Morvain I** — the current Ashen Father's grandfather. He didn't create the Edict, but he solved a problem the other kingdoms had: guilt.

The other five kingdoms knew, on some level, that what they were doing was wrong. Morvain I gave them theological permission. He declared the Edict "the Hand of the Named God, purging the unworthy." He turned genocide into scripture.

The Crucible doesn't directly exploit orcs (no slaves, no hunts). It does something worse: it makes exploitation *sacred*. Every other kingdom's atrocities are justified by Crucible theology.

### Culture & Daily Life
A theocracy governed by the Ashen Father and a council of Cardinals. The city is carved into a dormant volcano — the volcanic heat is considered the "breath of God." Everything is stone, basalt, and fire. Eternal flames burn in every public space.

**Worship:** The Named God is never depicted — only described. The central tenet: "All things have names, and names have power. The Named God spoke the names of the worthy races and did not speak the name of the orc." Orcs are therefore "the Unnamed" — theologically nonexistent.

**The Branded:** A fanatical order of human penitents who brand themselves with orc-style marks and infiltrate orc camps as spies. They consider this holy work — "walking among the Unnamed to ensure they remain docile." They're extremely dangerous because they can pass as orcs (from a distance) and they're willing to die for the mission.

### Target Expansions

**His Holiness, Ashen Father Morvain III (Edictbearer):**
Age 78. Frail, soft-spoken, kind-eyed. He prays twelve hours a day. He fasts three days a week. He genuinely, sincerely, with total conviction, believes that orc extinction is God's merciful plan.

He is not a hypocrite. He's not secretly cruel. He's the most dangerous kind of zealot — one who lives his values perfectly. He has never raised his voice. He has never struck another person. He has also never once questioned whether the theology that justifies genocide might be wrong.

Killing him feels wrong even though it's necessary. That's the point.

## Kingdom 6: The Accord Spire

### History
Built 85 years ago as neutral ground for the six-kingdom alliance. The Edict required cooperation — each kingdom's Edictbearer had to participate in an annual renewal ritual at the Spire. Over time, the Spire became the administrative heart of the alliance: trade agreements, border disputes, and Edict maintenance all run through it.

### Culture
Bureaucracy as architecture. The Spire is a vertical city — a tower so tall it disappears into clouds. Each level handles a different administrative function. The people who work here are clerks, diplomats, and administrators. They file paperwork about orc population decline using pre-printed forms. Genocide as office work.

### The Council of Scales
The sixth Edict-anchor is not in one person — it's distributed across the Council of Scales, six diplomats representing each kingdom. They must all die for the anchor to break. They never meet in one place (security protocol established after a previous assassination attempt 40 years ago).

This makes Kingdom 6 a puzzle-box: six targets in a single location who must be killed in a specific sequence before the others can flee or consolidate.

# SECTION IV: EDICTBEARER RELATIONSHIPS

The six Edictbearers are not isolated. They're bound by politics, trade, marriage, and the Edict itself. Killing one affects the others.

| Relationship | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Cowl ↔ Ashford** | Connected by marriage (Dame Cowl is from Ashford family). Greenhold provides food to Verdancy; Verdancy provides timber to Greenhold. Killing Cowl first causes Ashford to tighten security. |
| **Keelan ↔ Myre** | Trade partnership. Drench provides pearl-mineral compounds; Fane provides medical supplies for divers. Killing Keelan first causes Myre to accelerate research (looking for a non-pearl Edict-anchor). |
| **Myre ↔ Morvain** | Intellectual rivals. Myre is a rationalist; Morvain is a theologian. They despise each other but need each other — Myre provides the Edict's mechanism, Morvain provides its justification. Killing Myre first makes Morvain paranoid (increases Crucible security). Killing Morvain first makes Myre dismissive ("superstitious fool got what he deserved") and careless (decreases Fane security). |
| **Ashford ↔ Keelan** | Ashford hunts; Keelan extracts. Mutual respect between predators. If Ashford is killed, Keelan stations gnaw-hounds on his ship. |
| **Morvain ↔ Cowl** | Morvain blessed the Greenhold's labor system. Cowl tithes 10% of grain to the Crucible. If Cowl dies, Morvain launches an inquisition in the Greenhold, which creates chaos that can help OR hinder later operations. |
| **All ↔ Accord Spire** | All Edictbearers have a delegate on the Council of Scales. As Edictbearers die, their Council seats are not replaced — but the surviving delegates become more vigilant. The order you kill Edictbearers determines how difficult Kingdom 6 is. |
